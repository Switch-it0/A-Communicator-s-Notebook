Examples:

Another thing that I learned is how we express something. For me, it is a surprise that there's a correct way to do it but it is purposive communication.

Example:

When using negative (no, not), only use one at a time in any idea or expressing something, because you will create a double negative, which is an error.

Another unforgettable thing that I learned is how to use the Modal Verbs.

Modal Verbs

• Can / Could

• Should

• Would

Modal verbs like can, could, should, and would are essential for effective communication as they convey different meanings and intentions. The verb "can" is commonly used to express ability, permission, or possibility. For example, you might say, "I can complete the project by tomorrow," to indicate capability. Also the verb "could" is used like the verb "can" but it is more polite than the verb "can". For example, "Could we please go on a trip this weekend?".

Meanwhile, "should" is used to offer advice, express obligation, or reflect on something that might be expected. For instance, "You should study for the exam" suggests a recommendation or duty

On the other hand, "would" is versatile, often employed for politeness, hypothetical situations, or preferences. For example, "I would help you if I had more time" reflects a conditional statement, while "Would you like some coffee?" shows politeness in making an offer. Mastering these modal verbs enhances clarity and adds nuance to spoken and written communication.

Using these modal verbsappropriately ensures your communication is clear, respectful, and expressive.

Finally, this subject reminded me of the right use of four crucial proper punctuation marks.

Period (.)

The purpose is to mark the end of a declarative phrase or indirect question.

Comma (,)

The purpose is to indicate a pause, break down elements, or clarify meaning.

Apostrophe (')

The purpose demonstrates possession or forms contractions.

Quotation Mark ("")

The purpose is to include direct speech, titles, or quotes.

Learned during Class

Listening intently to Purposive Communication taught me many valuable lessons and skills. But there is something I haven't forgotten since we started midterms. I've learned the Yes/No Question and WH Question

After the preliminary of Purposive Communication, I am eager to study more because English is more sophisticated than you realize.

The first is the Yes or No Question. In that lesson, I learned how to turn a statement into a Yes or No question. It is not easy to convert since the converting procedure involves numerous steps, or it must be considered before conversion. Like these examples:

• If the statement has only one verb and the verb is a form of "be", simply switch the locations of the subject and verb. EXAMPLE: Matthew is a chef - Is Matthew a chef?

• If there are two verbs, simply reverse the locations of the subject and the first verb. EXAMPLE: Maria is driving a family car - Is Maria driving a family car?

• If there is one verb and the verb is not a form of "be," the process is more complex: Add "do" to the beginning of the sentence. The Levins live in that Apartment - Do the Levins live in that Apartment?

• If the main verb has a third-person singular -s, transfer it to "do," resulting in "does". For example, Lyca drives an automobile. Does Lyca drive a car?

• If the main verb is in the past tense, change it to "do" to make it "did." Example: Ken played basketball last week. Did Ken play basketball last week?

I also recall the "be verbs" from that lesson, which are used to indicate the subject's status. Example:

• Present tense am, are, is;

• Past tense was, were;

• Past participle been; and

• Present participle being;

The second tip is the WH Question. In that lesson, I learned how to turn a statement into a WH Question. If the Yes or No Question is a simple question, this type of question is not difficult, but you learned about it. Also, before converting this WH Question, analyze the converting method. Consider the following examples:

• To make a question using the predicate pattern, first form a yes- or no- question by inverting the subject and first auxiliary verb, then add the appropriate wh-word to the beginning of the sentence: The meeting was some time - When was the meeting?

• If there is no auxiliary (verb) and the verb is not "be", add "do" to the beginning of the sentence, then add the appropriate wh-question word. Be sure to transfer the tense and number from the main verb to the word "do".

Example:

• You want something. - What do you want?

• You went somewhere. (past tense) - Where did you go?

• She likes something. (3rd person, s) - What does she like?

Not only that, but there are subject patterns, predicate patterns, and the auxiliary verb, sometimes known as the helping verb:

Subject Pattern

Simply change the person or item being asked about with the proper wh-word. Example is Something is bothering you. - What bothers you?

Predicate Pattern

The formulation of wh-questions is determined by the presence of an auxiliary (helping) verb in the original sentence:I should have finished my homework- Who finished his/her homework?

Auxiliary Verb

A verb used with another verb (or two other verbs) in a verb phrase (which includes be, have, do, and modal verbs such as may, shall, will, can, and must) to demonstrate a verb's tense or construct a question.

Learned during Class

Listening carefully to Purposive Communication taught me many essential lessons and skill sets. But there is one thing I haven't forgotten since we started pre-finals. I've studied the Preposition of Place, Transitional Words, and Comparative and Superlative Adjectives.

Following the midterm for Purposive Communication, I am ready to learn more because English is more advanced than you know.

The first is the Preposition of the Place, which is similar to a locator or, in today's world, Waze and Google Maps. I only put it that way because it guides us through the domain of nouns. Others are familiar with them, although others are unsure how to use the words in, on, and at properly.

In: Use it to talk about a place that is inside a bigger space, such as a box, a house, a city, or a country. Also, use it with otherphysical locations.

Example

• My dog is in the box.

• in the mountains / the countryside / a valley / the forest

On: Use on to talk about location on a surface and for some types of public transport that we can have a movement. Also, used for lines (including rivers, borders, streets, etc.) and islands.

Example

• There's a market for computers onQuezon Ave.

• The plant is on the desk.

• You can't make phone calls on a plane.

At: Use at in many common phrases, especially when we are talking about a place for a specific activity, and use at for addresses or exact positions.

Example

• I'm working on my blog site at home today.

• He's waiting at the entrance.

The second point to consider is that transitional words are often overlooked in sentences. They may not be an obvious element of the writing process, but they are critical for producing well-structured and compelling work. They function like bridges, seamlessly transporting your thoughts from one location to another. According to what I learned, these are typical transitional words:

To indicate cause/effect or reason:

• Because

• Since

• Due to

To indicate an example:

• For example

• For instance

To indicate comparison/contrast

• But

• Although

• In comparison...

• In contrast...

To indicate time or sequence:

• At First

• Meanwhile

• Lastly/Finally

Lastly, Comparative and Superlative Adjectives are words that describe, identify, or quantify nouns and pronouns. They help specify our writing by offering more details about nouns and pronouns. But there are differences between the two:

Comparative Adjective:

• Used to compare two things they describe differences between two nouns.

• The suffix -er will be added for comparative adjectives.

• In some cases, the sentence will end after the comparative adjective and not include the object of comparison. This structure is possible when the context has provided enough information to make the comparison clear.

Examples:

• My television is bigger than my computer.

• My brother is 6 feet tall, but my father is taller.

Superlative Adjective:

• Used to compare three or more things. They help describe things on either end of a spectrum

• The suffix -est for superlative adjectives.

• The object of comparison, like comparative adjectives, might be absent sometimes.

Examples:

• My English professor is the smartest person.

• We took an exam in class today, and I scored the highest.

They are not easy to create a comparative and superlative but there are keywords the "than" for comparative and "the" for superlative but there are also in creating this adjective they are not always suffixes of -er and -est. The following are the tips:

One syllable adjective:

• When the adjective has a single vowel between two consonants (consonant + single vowel + consonant), the second consonant will be doubled. For example, Adjective = hot - h (consonant) + o (single vowel) + t (second consonant) = Comparative form: hotter and Superlative form: hottest.

Two-Syllable Adjectives

• For comparative adjectives, the suffix -er will be added or preceded by more. Superlative adjectives will be preceded by most or the suffix -est. When in doubt, use more or most rather than a suffix. For adjectives ending in y, the y will be replaced with an i, and the appropriate suffix will be added.

Three-Syllable Adjective:

Comparative adjectives will be preceded by nore, and superlative adjectives will be preceded by most.

An irregular adjective

• Are irregular if their comparative and superlative forms do not follow the standards outlined.

Tips & Examples:

• good, better, best.

• much, more, most.

• Avoid using "nore" with a comparative created with "-er" or "most" with a superlative produced with "-est.

• Stress that the "as [adjective] as" formulation is intended to express equality through comparison. For example, "as cute as" does not mean "cuter as."
